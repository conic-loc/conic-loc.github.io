Abstract

We present ConicLoc, a monocular visual localization (VL) method that leverages time-invariant, known-radius circular landmarks in structured environments to estimate six-DoF camera poses without a pre-built map. Existing map-based VL methods depend on handcrafted keypoints, semantic cues, or pre-built maps, making them vulnerable to illumination/appearance changes and frequent environmental reconfigurations. ConicLoc exploits circular structures' known radii as a compact metric prior together with Manhattan world (MW) regularities, requiring no depth sensing, learned descriptors, or a pre-built map. We first propose a Perspective-Two-Circle (P2C) solver that recovers a metric-scale initial pose from two detected ellipses with known radii. From P2C's conic normals, we infer dominant MW directions and integrate MW-aligned lines for drift-free orientation and low sensitivity to image noise. All poses are further refined via a conic bundle adjustment (CBA) minimizing algebraic conic residuals for global consistency. We evaluate ConicLoc on synthetic and real-world environments, including ISS free-flying robot sequences and nano-drone sequences under day–night illumination change. Without a pre-built map, ConicLoc achieves accuracy comparable to map-based VL methods while maintaining performance across severe illumination changes.

Contributions

01

We fully exploit structural conic geometry with a closed-form Perspective–Two–Circle (P2C) solver and a conic bundle adjustment (CBA) that globally optimizes algebraic ellipse residuals.

02

We identify and alleviate the noise sensitivity of monocular conic pose estimation by integrating Manhattan world (MW) constraints, and demonstrate its effectiveness through synthetic experiments.

03

We evaluate ConicLoc across two robotic platforms — an ISS free-flying robot (Astrobee) and a nano-drone (Crazyflie) — under day and night conditions, achieving accuracy comparable to map-based VL methods while remaining robust to illumination change.

0 · Monocular Image

A single RGB frame from the onboard camera (Astrobee or Crazyflie), containing two visible circular landmarks — e.g., a hatch and a window — as the sole geometric input. No depth sensor or stereo pair is required.

1 · Ellipse & Line Feature Extraction

A YOLO-based detector localizes the two circular landmarks, followed by ellipse fitting to recover each conic's image-plane parameters. In parallel, an LSD line detector extracts candidate line segments for the Manhattan-world stage.

Example detection result — coming soon

2 · P2C Solver with Manhattan-World Line Optimization

Each detected ellipse defines a tangent-ray cone back-projected from the camera center. Eigen-decomposing this cone yields a closed-form, metric-scale 3D center and surface normal for each circle. Since both circles lie on the same rigid panel, the two-fold P2C ambiguity is resolved by selecting the solution pair whose normals align most strongly. The recovered dominant plane then anchors a RANSAC-based Manhattan-world line optimization that refines the camera rotation for drift-free, noise-robust orientation.

Example P2C geometry — coming soon

3 · Conic Bundle Adjustment (CBA)

All per-frame poses are jointly refined by minimizing algebraic conic residuals — whether reprojected 3D circle points satisfy the detected ellipse's implicit quadratic equation — via Levenberg–Marquardt, enforcing global geometric consistency without explicit point-to-point correspondences.

4 · 6-DoF Camera Pose

The final output: a metric-scale camera rotation and translation in the world frame, refined by conic bundle adjustment. This pose is what downstream navigation and control modules consume — no map or learned model is carried forward between frames.

Datasets & Video Results

ConicLoc is evaluated on three complementary settings: a controlled synthetic noise study that isolates the contribution of each geometric cue, a Crazyflie nano-drone day–night dataset that stresses illumination robustness, and the Astrobee ISS free-flyer dataset that stresses real robotic operation inside a structured, low-texture environment.

Synthetic Noise Study

Noise-free ellipse observations are generated by projecting 3D circular structures onto a virtual camera, then zero-mean Gaussian noise is independently injected into the ellipse centroid, axes, and orientation across a range of noise levels σ ∈ [0, 10]. The ellipse-only pipeline degrades sharply once noise exceeds a moderate level, while incorporating Manhattan-world line cues keeps both ATE and ARE stable even under strong noise (σ = 10).

Crazyflie Day–Night Localization

An author-collected dataset captured with a Crazyflie nano-drone near a building equipped with two circular windows. A reference map is built from day images only; ConicLoc is then localized against separate day and night query sequences without ever rebuilding the map or retraining a model.

Astrobee ISS Free-Flyer

ConicLoc is evaluated on Astrobee free-flyer sequences recorded inside the ISS JEM (Kibo) module, under both original and artificially darkened illumination, using only a hatch and a window as circular landmarks.

Limitations

ConicLoc relies on circular landmarks with known radii, exhibiting some scene dependency; however, such structures and their dimensions are commonly available or measurable in engineered environments. It also assumes circle normals aligned with the Manhattan-world axes, well suited to structured interiors; extending to arbitrarily oriented circles is a promising direction for future work.
